What to think about to justify a purchase in edge cases. Ask, “Is it…
* True novelty, but intuitive and easy to understand. Vermont Chewman, Furminator, Greenies
* Something many pet owners need, whether they know it yet or not. Furminator
* Ability to test with a reasonable amount/investment.
* Not redundant or cannibalistic with other current products.
   * If redundant, is it a significant improvement and better value to what we have? So much that we can get rid of the older product?
      * This could be for us or the customer. IE Easier to buy, better quality, value to customer, easier to use, solves a problem better
      * We have to overcome existing brand recognition with obvious improvement. IE Nature’s Miracle/Petastic
   * OR the market is eager for additional capacity or options?
* Does it replace a good product that sold well that we can no longer get?
   * Why can’t we get the other product? Low adoption? Not a sustainable business or product?
   * Priced out of our market?
      * Big box sells near what we buy for?
* High volume sales, consumable, above average profit.
   * Line extension
   * Brand recognition
   * Strong and growing department
* Does it have market support, as in many customers asking for it by name. Or great merchandising POP, like engaging video, display, presentation?
   * Manufacturer advertising or ad dollars to support Braxton’s
   * Committed vendor support. Demos, consumer promos.

Different departments take different thinking


We should be cautious, even slow to adopt
* Dog and cat food and anything that has a high threshold for change, has inertia, and pain of disconnect:
   * In certain departments of inventory there is inertia, it takes longer for people to adopt and buy.
      * The customer has to be educated about the purpose or reason to switch buying habits.
      * There has to be a compelling reason and generally takes a long time to build a brand loyalty and following.
   * The opposite is if we have to discontinue a food that we have decided to carry, there is a pain of disconnection. 
      * Often there are a few customers who don’t want to switch off the food, causing us to make some hard decisions and upsetting those customers, keeping just a few skus on hand for them, or shifting to a subscription order. The later two are less desirable.
It's easier in more flexible departments
* Treats and toys are good examples:
   * Customers are willing to buy as impulse
   * Less brand loyalty, less need to stay with the same
   * Can be seasonal, or temporary “in and out” 
   * Less issue with elimination of product or line
   * Typically lower initial/test investment of time, energy, and money


Developing a strong Distributor Rep relationship through a period of exclusivity as a benefit of bringing the right products to our attention and work to get a new product or line(especially food)
* Exclusive for X months or a trigger to move beyond
   * Fill rate
   * Massive price change, (allow to counter)
   * Rep problems or change of rep
